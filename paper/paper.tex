\subsection*{ESEB Abstract}

\subsubsection*{Background}
Meiotic recombination during gamete formation is a fundamental evolutionary process. It is required to ensure proper chromosome segregation and promotes genetic diversity by shuffling alleles into novel haplotypes. This can create beneficial allele combinations and break up linkage between beneficial and harmful alleles, thereby facilitating adaptive evolution. However, high recombination rates also carry risks, such as introducing mutations at crossover sites, and potentially disrupting favorable allele combinations. Thus, recombination rates could affect individual fitness in opposite directions, suggesting a complex relationship with individual fitness.

Investigating the impact of recombination rates on fitness is complicated by the fact that identifying crossover positions during meiosis - and thus finding the crossover count - is only possible for individuals with genotyped offspring. This introduces a sampling bias which prevents measuring the recombination rates of individuals that are reproductively unsuccessful, excluding them from potential fitness analyses. Failure to account for these unmeasured individuals could potentially bias analyses of the fitness effects of recombination rates.

\subsubsection*{Materials and methods}
We used a long-term house sparrow study system from northern Norway with extensive pedigree, genotype, and fitness data to investigate the relationship between individual recombination rates and fitness components. The genomic and pedigree data were leveraged to measure autosomal crossover count (ACC) in adult house sparrows, resulting in one ACC measure per genotyped parent-offspring pair. We estimated sex-specific individual genetic values for ACC by using the phenotypic ACC measures for each parent as responses in a genomic prediction model. The genomic prediction, implemented as a Bayesian G-BLUP model, allowed us to estimate these genetic values in more than 2,600 phenotyped individuals, as well as in more than 2,900 additional individuals with no recorded offspring, correcting the aforementioned sampling bias and increasing the sample size of the subsequent fitness analyses.

We then related the estimated genetic values to sex-specific fitness components, including annual reproductive success and annual survival. As the individuals without ACC measurements had higher uncertainty in their estimated genetic values, we used measurement error modelling to account for the heterogeneous genetic value uncertainties. 

\subsubsection*{Results}
We found that individual ACC was related to male and female annual survival, with predicted annual survival peaking at intermediate genetic values for individual ACC. We also found weak evidence that males with intermediate genetic values had higher annual recruit production, but no evidence of a relationship with reproductive success in females. Altogether these results suggest potential stabilizing selection against extreme values of ACC, especially in male house sparrows.

\subsubsection*{Conclusions}
We demonstrate that individual variation in recombination rates influences fitness in a wild house sparrow population, with evidence of stabilizing selection on ACC particularly in male house sparrows. To interpret this association, we consider possible underlying biological mechanisms. On the methodological side, we present genomic prediction as a tool to address sampling biases. This novel approach allowed us to investigate the fitness consequences of a trait that can only be directly measured in reproductively successful individuals and allowed us to include several thousand individuals in the analysis that would otherwise have been excluded.

\subsection*{Genomic Prediction}

We performed GP of ACC with a Bayesian linear mixed with a Gaussian likelihood, fitted in \texttt{R-INLA} \citep{rue2009approximate}.
While ACC is a count trait, its rates were high enough that assuming a continuous Gaussian likelihood was a reasonable modelling assumption.
Given ACC measurements $y_{ij}$ of individual $i$ estimated from offspring $j$, we modelled
\begin{equation}
    y_{ij} = \mu + \beta_\text{coverage} \cdot x_{\text{coverage},ij} + g_i + z_{\text{hy},i} + z_{\text{hi},i} + z_{\text{id},i} + z_{\varepsilon,ij}\ ,
    \label{eq-gp}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is an intercept, $\beta_\text{coverage}$ is the regression coefficient for total coverage $x_{\text{coverage},ij}$, and $z_{\text{hy},i}$, $z_{\text{hi},i}$, $z_{\text{id},i}$, and $z_{\varepsilon,ij}$ are unstructured random effects corresponding to hatch year, (known or presumed) hatch island, permanent environmental effects and residual environmental effects, respectively.
The genetic values $g_i$ form a structured random effect, such that the vector $\bm g = \left[g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_N\right]$ follows the distribution $\N{\bm{0}}{\sigma_{\text{G}}^2 \mathbf{G}}$, where $\sigma_{\text{G}}^2$ is the additive genetic variance and $\mathbf{G}$ is a pre-computed genomic relatedness matrix estimated with the first of the formulas given by \citet{vanraden2008efficient}.
We placed independent normal priors $\N{0}{10^3}$ on fixed effect coefficients, and penalized complexity priors PC$\left(\hat \sigma / \sqrt{2}, 0.05\right)$ on the random effect variances \citep{simpson2017penalising}, $\hat\sigma$ being the sample standard deviation of $y_{ij}$.
Fitting the GP model produced a joint posterior distribution for the entries of the genetic value vector $\bm g$, which we used in the following fitness analyses.
We fitted separate GP models corresponding to each fitness model described in the following.

We investigated the impact of the genetic values for sex-specific ACC genetic values $g_i$ on three different fitness components: annual reproductive success (ARS), annual survival (AS), and nestling survival (NS).
Similar to the genetic values $g_i$, all modelling of ARS, AS, and NS was done separately for males and females.
As detailed below, we first related the ARS and AS of an individual $i$ to its own ACC genetic value $g_i$, and next to the genetic value $g_{p(i)}$ of its same-sex parent $p(i)$ in a separate model.
We label the former as \textit{adult} models and the latter as \textit{parental} models.
For nestling $i$ we only related $g_{p(i)}$ to the NS of $i$, similar to the parental models for ARS and AS.
We modelled all three fitness traits with Bayesian GLMMs as detailed below.
