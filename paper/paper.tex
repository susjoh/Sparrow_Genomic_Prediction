\subsection*{ESEB Abstract}

\subsubsection*{Background}
Meiotic recombination during gamete formation is a fundamental evolutionary process. It is required to ensure proper chromosome segregation and promotes genetic diversity by shuffling alleles into novel haplotypes. This can create beneficial allele combinations and break up linkage between beneficial and harmful alleles, thereby facilitating adaptive evolution. However, high recombination rates also carry risks, such as introducing mutations at crossover sites, and potentially disrupting favorable allele combinations. Thus, recombination rates could affect individual fitness in opposite directions, suggesting a complex relationship with individual fitness.

Investigating the impact of recombination rates on fitness is complicated by the fact that identifying crossover positions during meiosis - and thus finding the crossover count - is only possible for individuals with genotyped offspring. This introduces a sampling bias which prevents measuring the recombination rates of individuals that are reproductively unsuccessful, excluding them from potential fitness analyses. Failure to account for these unmeasured individuals could potentially bias analyses of the fitness effects of recombination rates.

\subsubsection*{Materials and methods}
We used a long-term house sparrow study system from northern Norway with extensive pedigree, genotype, and fitness data to investigate the relationship between individual recombination rates and fitness components. The genomic and pedigree data were leveraged to measure autosomal crossover count (ACC) in adult house sparrows, resulting in one ACC measure per genotyped parent-offspring pair. We estimated sex-specific individual genetic values for ACC by using the phenotypic ACC measures for each parent as responses in a genomic prediction model. The genomic prediction, implemented as a Bayesian G-BLUP model, allowed us to estimate these genetic values in more than 2,600 phenotyped individuals, as well as in more than 2,900 additional individuals with no recorded offspring, correcting the aforementioned sampling bias and increasing the sample size of the subsequent fitness analyses.

We then related the estimated genetic values to sex-specific fitness components, including annual reproductive success and annual survival. As the individuals without ACC measurements had higher uncertainty in their estimated genetic values, we used measurement error modelling to account for the heterogeneous genetic value uncertainties. 

\subsubsection*{Results}
We found that individual ACC was related to male and female annual survival, with predicted annual survival peaking at intermediate genetic values for individual ACC. We also found weak evidence that males with intermediate genetic values had higher annual recruit production, but no evidence of a relationship with reproductive success in females. Altogether these results suggest potential stabilizing selection against extreme values of ACC, especially in male house sparrows.

\subsubsection*{Conclusions}
We demonstrate that individual variation in recombination rates influences fitness in a wild house sparrow population, with evidence of stabilizing selection on ACC particularly in male house sparrows. To interpret this association, we consider possible underlying biological mechanisms. On the methodological side, we present genomic prediction as a tool to address sampling biases. This novel approach allowed us to investigate the fitness consequences of a trait that can only be directly measured in reproductively successful individuals and allowed us to include several thousand individuals in the analysis that would otherwise have been excluded.

\subsection*{Outline of fitness analyses}

We investigated how the number of autosomal crossovers impacted fitness in our study populations.
We addressed this by asking two related questions: first, how the ACC in the gametes an individual received from its parents impacted its fitness, and second, how an individual's own recombination rate impacted its fitness.
In both analyses, we used mixed modelling to relate ACC to fitness components, including annual reproductive success (ARS), annual adult survival (AS), and nestling survival (NS).
However, the second analysis was complicated by sampling biases, which we accounted for by applying genomic prediction (GP) and measurement error modelling.
We fitted all models separately for the sexes since male and female ACC in house sparrows are essentially different traits with low genetic correlations \citep{mcauley2024genetic}, and since the dynamics of selection could be fundamentally different between the sexes \note{(cite some house sparrow paper)}.

\subsection*{Fitness impacts of ACC in inherited gametes}

We related the ACC in an individual's maternal and paternal gametes to that individual's (sex-specific) fitness using generalized linear mixed models for ARS, AS and NS, fitted in a Bayesian framework using \texttt{R-INLA} \citep{rue2009approximate}.
The ACC information was incorporated in two different ways. 
In what we label the \textit{sire and dam} (\textit{S\&D}) models, the ACCs in the gametes from the sire and dam were treated as two separate fixed effects in the same model, while in the \textit{parental sum} (\textit{PS}) models we used the sum of the ACC in the sire's and dam's gametes as a single covariate.
The S\&D models therefore allowed for the possibility that the egg-derived and sperm-derived ACCs have different fitness effects, as a form of genomic imprinting \note{(add some citation)}, with these effects estimated conditional on each other.
Conversely, the PS models considered the effect of overall inherited ACC, irrespective of source parent.
Below we outline the \textit{S\&D} and \textit{PS} models for the three fitness traits.

\subsubsection*{Annual reproductive success}

ARS was modelled with a zero-inflated Poisson likelihood, so that $\text{ARS}_{ij}$, the number of recruits produced by individual $i$ in its $j^\text{th}$ adult year, was given by
\begin{align} % Zero-inflated Poisson GLMM
\begin{split}
\label{eq-ars}
\mathrm{ARS}_{ij} &\sim
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{with probability } \pi,\\[-10pt]
\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_{ij}), & \text{with probability } 1-\pi,
\end{cases}
\\[-5pt]
\operatorname{logit}(\pi) &= \gamma_0,
\\[-10pt]
\log(\lambda_{ij}) &= \eta_{ij},
\end{split}
\end{align}
where $\lambda_{ij}$ is the Poisson rate linked to the linear predictor $\eta_{ij}$ by a log link, and $\pi$ is the zero-inflation probability determined by a single intercept term $\gamma_0$.
For both the \textit{S\&D} and \textit{PS} models, $\eta_{ij}$ included an intercept, fixed effects for age (linear and quadratic) and inbreeding coefficient (linear), and unstructured random effects of measurement year, measurement island, individual ID, dam, sire, and a residual random effect.
% age: polynomial bases
% inbreeding: froh (Burnett et al)
In the \textit{S\&D} models, $\eta_{ij}$ also contained fixed effects of ACC in the dam gamete (linear and quadratic), ACC in the sire gamete (linear and quadratic), whereas in the \textit{PS} models $\eta_{ij}$ contained the effect of the sum of the ACC in the parental gametes (linear and quadratic).
We placed a Gaussian prior $\N{0}{1.75^2}$ on $\gamma_0$, wide independent Gaussian priors $\N{0}{10^4}$ on the fixed effect coefficients, and independent penalized complexity priors $\text{PC}(1, 0.05)$ on the random effect variances \citep{simpson2017penalising}.

\subsubsection*{Annual Survival}

We modelled AS as a binomial trait with a logistic link, so that
\begin{align}
\begin{split}
\label{eq-as}
\mathrm{AS}_{ij} &\sim
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{with probability } 1 - p_{ij},\\[-10pt]
1, & \text{with probability } p_{ij},
\end{cases}
\\[-5pt]
\operatorname{logit}(p_{ij}) &= \eta_{ij},
\end{split}
\end{align}
where $\mathrm{AS}_{ij}$ is an indicator for whether individual $i$ survived its $j^\text{th}$ adult year, and $\eta_{ij}$ is a linear predictor linked to the survival probability $p_{ij}$ by a logit link.
For both the \textit{S\&D} and \textit{PS} models, we included the same fixed and random effects in $\eta_{ij}$ as in the model for ARS described above, with the same prior specifications.

\subsection*{Fitness impacts of own ACCs}

\subsubsection*{Sampling bias in ACC measurements}

To investigate the fitness consequences of an individual's recombination rate, we could in principle have related the ACCs in the gametes produced by individual $i$ to directly the fitness of $i$.
However, since we were only able to measure ACC for genotyped individuals with \textit{known and genotyped offspring}, the individuals that were reproductively unsuccessful were systematically unmeasured for ACC.
Thus, a fitness analysis excluding these individual would thus suffer from a systematic sampling bias, where less fit individuals would be missing not at random.

As a workaround to this sampling bias, we used GP to predict genetic values of ACC for \textit{all} genotyped individuals with fitness data, including non-breeders.
Thus, by considering the fitness effects of the predicted additive genetic component of ACC, rather than the unevenly sampled ACC measurements, we eliminated this sampling bias.
Below we outline a Bayesian GP model that estimated and predicted sex-specific ACC genetic values.
We then related these genetic values to ARS and AS, while appropriately accounting for the uncertainty in our genetic value estimates using measurement error modelling.

\subsubsection*{Genomic Prediction of ACC}

We performed GP of ACC with a Bayesian linear mixed model with a Gaussian likelihood, fitted in \texttt{R-INLA} \citep{rue2009approximate}.
While ACC is a count trait, its rates were high enough that assuming a continuous Gaussian likelihood was a reasonable modelling assumption.
Given the ACC measurement $\text{ACC}_{ij}$ of the gamete that individual $i$ passed  to offspring $j$, we modelled
\begin{equation}
    \text{ACC}_{ij} = \mu + \beta_\text{coverage} \cdot x_{\text{coverage},ij} + \beta_f f_i +  g_i + z_{\text{hy},i} + z_{\text{hi},i} + z_{\text{id},i} + z_{\varepsilon,ij}\ ,
    \label{eq-gp}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is an intercept, $\beta_\text{coverage}$ is the regression coefficient for total coverage $x_{\text{coverage},ij}$, $\beta_f$ is the regression coefficient for inbreeding coefficient $f_i$ of individual $i$ and $z_{\text{hy},i}$, $z_{\text{hi},i}$, $z_{\text{id},i}$, and $z_{\varepsilon,ij}$ are unstructured random effects corresponding to hatch year, (known or presumed) hatch island, permanent environmental effects and residual environmental effects, respectively.
The genetic values $g_i$ form a structured random effect, such that the vector $\bm g = \left(g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_N\right)^\top$ follows the distribution $\N{\bm{0}}{\sigma_{\text{G}}^2 \mathbf{G}}$, where $\sigma_{\text{G}}^2$ is the additive genetic variance and $\mathbf{G}$ is a pre-computed genomic relatedness matrix estimated with the first of the formulas given by \citet{vanraden2008efficient}.
We had $0$ to $42$ measurements of $\text{ACC}_{ij}$ per individual $i$, but only estimated one genetic value $g_i$.
Therefore, $g_i$ should be interpreted as the genetic propensity of individual $i$ to produce gametes with high ACC compared to the overall population, and thus reflects that individual's underlying recombination rate.
We placed independent normal priors $\N{0}{10^4}$ on fixed effect coefficients, and penalized complexity priors PC$\left(\hat \sigma / \sqrt{2}, 0.05\right)$ on the random effect variances \citep{simpson2017penalising}, $\hat\sigma$ being the sample standard deviation of $y_{ij}$.
Fitting the GP model produced a joint posterior distribution for the entries of the genetic value vector $\bm g$, which we used in the subsequent fitness analyses.

\subsubsection*{Fitness analyses using ACC breeding values}

We fitted models for ARS and AS that used the posterior estimates of $g_i$ from the model in Equation \eqref{eq-gp} as the covariate of interest.
These models had a similar form to the previously described models in Equations \eqref{eq-ars} and \eqref{eq-as}.
However, we could not straightforwardly replace the ACC covariates from the \textit{S\&D} models and \textit{PS} models with a linear and quadratic effect of $g_i$, since the uncertainty in $g_i$ needs to be propagated from the GP into the fitness models.
Fitting the Bayesian GP model \eqref{eq-gp} gave us a full joint posteriors for the genetic value vector $\bm g$, which was approximately multivariate Gaussian.
In the fitness models, we could therefore incorporate the measurement error in these genetic value by sampling genetic values from Gaussian approximations of these posterior distributions.
